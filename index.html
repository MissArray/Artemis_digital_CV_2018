A bit about me

Present

I want to make things that look good, work well and solve problems.

If I can solve a problem, I tend to go for solutions that are both practical and elegant, given the choice. At some point, this tendency grew into a need to create things that are functional, aesthetically appealing and make someone’s life somehow easier. This need, in turn, became a driver of career change.

Coding provides both the tools and the raw material to build such things.

When I started exploring new career routes, I was lucky enough to come across Founders and Coders – and something clicked: coding, I realised, can give me the tools and raw material with which to build smart solutions to users’ problems and take me places I never thought I’d get to explore.

The intersection of art, science, technology and society is a fascinating place. Web design and UX/UI design span all of these domains.

My fascination with the point at which art, science, technology and society intersect and interact and my inclination to dabble with graphics led me rather naturally to web and UX/UI design, in which I started training myself systematically as soon as I graduated from the coding bootcamp. Why? Because the broader field of web design is orientated to people’s digital needs and integrates research, creativity, functionality and problem-solving with the malleability and versatility of coding tools. It's also hugely enjoyable (for me at least) even when it poses technical riddles I don’t know how to solve – to start with.

Beautiful web design can only be excellent web design if it’s practical for users and practicable for developers.

As I understand it, web and UX/UI design functions in itself as an interface between clients, developers and users. Thanks to the Founders and Coders course, I now have a fair grasp of what goes on at both the back and the front end: apart from progressing constantly in HTML5 and CSS3, I am familiar with JavaScript, Node.js, PostgreSQL, Express and React, I use Git regularly and I’ve learned the Agile methodology. This means that I can understand and take into account the needs of both users and developers. I have also attended introductory workshops in Elixir and Haskell because, although smitten with web design, I am open to other possibilities (and perhaps a little addicted to learning).

Being active and giving back to the community matters to me.

I’m still involved with Founders and Coders as one of four pastoral carers for the current students and have also prepared and delivered to the current class an introduction to web typography (jointly with a fellow graduate) and an introduction to wireframes (on my own).

Past

I started out as a translator and copy editor. That stretched only one part of my brain; web design and coding offer me full brain circuit-training. But I came out of my previous career equipped with a versatile skill set – and I’m taking it with me.

In terms of coding, I started literally from scratch. In terms of knowing

  • how to research, analyse, synthesise and present data
  • how to assess the feasibility of a project
  • how to plan a realistic schedule
  • how to handle clients
  • how to work methodically
  • how to keep a cool head when the heat is on
  • how to document everything
  • how to structure reports and essays

I have a head start. I’m grateful for what my past career has taught me, but copy-editing, even at its most demanding, only ever took me down the same path. I’ve now started exploring different territories - and these tools are proving very handy, particularly in UX/UI design, where researching user needs is key.

Future

I’m looking forward to chances to work with experienced web developers and web and UX/UI designers, learn new technologies, tackle real-life problems with responsive, accessible, cross-browser products and promote the ethical use of IT. This seems to me like a pretty good way of spending the best part of each working day.
